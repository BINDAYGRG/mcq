Week 1: Introduction to International Strategic Marketing

Academic Theory: The Multidimensional Challenge

International marketing (defined by Hollensen) is the execution of one or more marketing mix decisions across national boundaries. Unlike domestic marketing, it introduces extreme complexity due to multidimensional risk (currency exchange, foreign legal systems, and cultural friction).

The core strategic debate is Standardization vs. Adaptation.

  • Standardization is driven by the desire for economies of scale in R&D, production, and marketing. It creates a unified global brand identity but risks alienating local consumers.
  • Adaptation is driven by differences in consumer behavior, purchasing power, and strict local regulations.

The modern solution is Glocalization ("Think Global, Act Local"), which seeks to balance the cost-saving benefits of global integration with the consumer appeal of local responsiveness. A firm's approach is often dictated by the EPRG Framework:

  • Ethnocentric: Home-country standard is applied everywhere.
  • Polycentric: Every host country gets a highly adapted strategy.
  • Geocentric: A world-oriented view targeting global segments with standardized core products but localized marketing.

💡 Layman's Translation

Selling in your home country is playing checkers; selling globally is playing 3D chess. The biggest headache is deciding whether to sell the exact same product everywhere (Standardization) to save a ton of money, or to tweak the product for every single country (Adaptation) so locals actually buy it. Glocalization is the ultimate compromise: build the expensive, hidden parts of the product the same way everywhere, but change the outside features and the advertisements so they fit the local vibe perfectly.

🌍 Real-Life Scenario

Consider a global fast-food giant. To achieve economies of scale, they standardize their kitchen equipment, point-of-sale software, and supply chain logistics globally. However, to survive in a market like India, they completely adapt the menu (Actual Product) to exclude beef and include local spices, while their advertising (Communication) focuses heavily on multi-generational family dining rather than quick solo meals.

Week 2: International Market Environment

Academic Theory: Environmental Scanning Frameworks

Before committing resources to a foreign market, firms must conduct multi-layered environmental scanning.

  • Macro-Environment (PESTEL): Evaluates uncontrollable external forces. (Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental, Legal).
  • Meso-Environment (Porter’s Five Forces): Assesses the attractiveness and profit potential of a specific industry. If barriers to entry are low, buyer/supplier power is high, and substitutes are plentiful, industry rivalry will be fierce and profits will be low.
  • National Advantage (Porter’s Diamond): Explains why certain nations achieve global dominance in specific industries. It relies on four interacting pillars: Factor Conditions, Demand Conditions, Related/Supporting Industries, and Firm Strategy/Rivalry.

💡 Layman's Translation

Before you spend millions entering a new country, scan the battlefield. PESTEL is the satellite view. Porter's Five Forces is the drone view. Porter's Diamond looks at the soil—asking why countries like Germany are naturally incredible at making cars.

Week 3: International Product Decisions

Academic Theory: Product Levels and Constraints

International product policy requires dissecting the offering into three tiers:

  1. Core Product: The fundamental, intangible problem-solving benefit.
  2. Actual Product: The physical attributes, packaging, quality level, and brand name.
  3. Augmented Product: After-sales service, warranties, installation, and delivery networks.

When taking a product abroad, adaptations fall into two categories:

  • Mandatory Adaptation: Changes dictated by absolute legal, safety, or geographical requirements.
  • Discretionary (Optional) Adaptation: Changes made to better align with local tastes.

Marketers must leverage or mitigate the Country of Origin (COO) Effect—the bias where consumers utilize manufacture country as a heuristic for quality.

Week 4: Socio-Cultural Considerations

Academic Theory: The Invisible Matrix of Consumer Behavior

Hofstede’s Cultural Dimensions remain the premier framework for cross-cultural analysis:

  • Power Distance (PDI): Acceptance of unequal power distribution.
  • Individualism vs. Collectivism (IDV): Self-reliance vs. group harmony.
  • Masculinity vs. Femininity (MAS): Achievement/material reward vs. cooperation/quality of life.
  • Uncertainty Avoidance (UAI): Tolerance for ambiguity and risk.

Marketers must also navigate Edward T. Hall’s concept of High-Context vs. Low-Context cultures. The ultimate threat is the Self-Reference Criterion (SRC)—the unconscious tendency to evaluate a foreign market through the lens of one's own cultural norms.

Weeks 5 & 6: Market Selection & Entry Modes

Academic Theory: Screening and The Risk/Control Continuum

Firms use the MACS Matrix to score market attractiveness against competitive strength. SMEs often use an opportunistic approach based on low Psychic Distance.

Entry Modes exist on a continuum balancing Risk and Control:

  1. Exporting: Low risk, low control.
  2. Licensing & Franchising: Moderate risk and return.
  3. Joint Ventures (JV) & Strategic Alliances: Shared risk, high potential for conflict.
  4. Foreign Direct Investment (FDI): Maximum control, maximum financial and political risk.

Week 7: International Communication Strategies

Academic Theory: Encoding, Decoding, and Noise

The foundational communication model dictates that a Sender must Encode a message into symbols, send it through a media channel, and the Receiver must Decode it. Noise is any interference (bad translation, cultural taboos) that distorts the message.

  • Push Strategy: Focusing marketing efforts on channel intermediaries.
  • Pull Strategy: Focusing heavily on mass media advertising to the end-consumer.

Week 8: International Pricing Strategies

Pricing is the only "P" that generates revenue. International pricing is distinctly complex:

  • Price Escalation: Disproportionate retail price increase of exports due to tariffs, shipping, and middlemen.
  • Transfer Pricing: Internal price charged between affiliated subsidiaries across borders.
  • Grey Markets: Unauthorized distributors exploiting cross-border price differences.

Week 9: Global Market Research

The core objective is to identify opportunities and mitigate financial risk.

  • Secondary Data: Pre-existing data. Mandatory first step, cheap but may be outdated.
  • Primary Data: Original research. Highly accurate but expensive.

Cross-cultural pitfalls include Conceptual Equivalence, Translation Errors (requires Back-Translation), and sampling issues in developing nations.
