Week 1: Intro & Glocalization

Introduction to International Strategic Marketing

Academic Theory: The Multidimensional Challenge

International marketing is the execution of one or more marketing mix decisions across national boundaries. The core strategic debate is Standardization vs. Adaptation.

  • Standardization: Driven by economies of scale. Creates unified global brand but risks alienating locals.
  • Adaptation: Driven by consumer differences and strict local regulations.

The modern solution is Glocalization ("Think Global, Act Local"). Approach is dictated by the EPRG Framework (Ethnocentric, Polycentric, Geocentric).

💡 Layman's Translation

Selling locally is checkers; selling globally is 3D chess. Glocalization is the ultimate compromise: build the expensive, hidden parts of the product the same way everywhere, but change the outside features and ads so they fit the local vibe.

International Market Environment

Academic Theory: Environmental Scanning

Firms conduct multi-layered scanning before entry.

  • Macro (PESTEL): Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental, Legal. Evaluates uncontrollable external forces.
  • Meso (Porter’s Five Forces): Assesses industry profit potential (Rivalry, Substitutes, New Entrants, Buyer/Supplier power).
  • National Advantage (Porter’s Diamond): Explains why nations achieve dominance (e.g., German cars via demanding buyers and strong supply chains).

💡 Layman's Translation

PESTEL is the satellite view—checking the country's economy. Porter's Five Forces is the drone view—looking at direct rivals to see if the market is too crowded.

International Product Decisions

Academic Theory: Product Levels

  1. Core: Fundamental benefit.
  2. Actual: Physical attributes and brand.
  3. Augmented: Warranties and delivery networks.

Adaptations are either Mandatory (legal/safety) or Discretionary (taste/preferences). Beware the Country of Origin (COO) Effect.

Socio-Cultural Considerations

Academic Theory: The Invisible Matrix

Hofstede’s Dimensions: Power Distance, Individualism/Collectivism, Masculinity/Femininity, Uncertainty Avoidance.

Beware the Self-Reference Criterion (SRC)—evaluating foreign markets through your own cultural lens.

Market Selection & Entry Modes

Screening uses the MACS Matrix (Market Attractiveness / Competitive Strength). Entry modes balance Risk vs Control:

  1. Exporting: Low risk, low control.
  2. Licensing/Franchising: Moderate risk.
  3. Joint Ventures: Shared risk, high conflict potential.
  4. FDI (Wholly Owned Subsidiary): Max control, max financial risk.

International Communication Strategies

Communication faces Noise (cultural taboos, translations). Strategies are either Push (focusing on wholesalers) or Pull (mass media to consumers). Ad campaigns can be standardized globally or heavily adapted.

International Pricing Strategies

  • Price Escalation: Massive price increase abroad due to tariffs/shipping.
  • Grey Markets: Unauthorized arbitrage due to price gaps between countries.
  • Geocentric Pricing: Baseline global price with coordinated local adjustments.

Global Market Research

Always begin with Secondary Data (fast, cheap). If inadequate, move to Primary Data (surveys). Beware Conceptual Equivalence and Translation Errors (always use Back-Translation).
